The game

A world that keeps moving.

Imperium Mortis is being built around the space between a lone RPG run and a faction-scale strategy game. You can stay close to one unit, or pull back and command more lives when you have earned them.

The world is meant to push back. Settlements have routines. Factions have grudges. Injuries, prisoners, hunger, reputation, and death are not background flavor when they can become the story.

01

Nobody starts important

You are not the chosen one. You begin as an exile, vagabond, refugee, or nobody at all. Most people are stronger, wealthier, better trained, and better connected than you. Survival comes before ambition.

02

Preparation wins battles

Most deaths happen long before the fighting starts. The wrong equipment, too little food, a missing torch, untreated wounds, or a poor route can doom an expedition before the first weapon is drawn.

03

Combat demands attention

A fight can change in seconds. Bleeding allies need treatment. Poison spreads. Units panic. Enemies exploit weakness. Victory depends on reacting before problems become disasters.

04

The world keeps moving

Settlements trade, travelers move, factions feud, and wars begin whether you are present or not. Opportunities disappear. Threats grow. The world does not wait for the player to catch up.

05

Scars remain

Lost limbs do not magically return. Dead companions stay dead. Failed expeditions leave consequences behind. Every victory and every mistake becomes part of the story that follows.

06

Strength is earned

There are no levels handed out for existing. Skills improve through use, labor builds endurance, and experience comes from surviving situations that could have ended your journey.

07

Build something worth defending

Purchase property, establish workshops, recruit followers, or claim land of your own. Every wall, workshop, and storehouse represents effort that can be lost if neglected.

08

Choose your place in history

The Empire lies shattered after a necromantic catastrophe at the old capital. Restore what was lost, profit from the chaos, found a kingdom, or ignore the struggle entirely.

The world

After the capital fell.

Necromancy is banned because everyone remembers what it cost. The old capital is surrounded by deathlands, and the people who survived still argue over who deserves to inherit the wreckage.

Human houses talk about restoration while raising armies for their own names. Orcs respect power without dressing it up. Elven courts carry old history, old mercy, and losses that did not end just because the songs did.

Creator

Domormu

Domormu is the sole developer behind Imperium Mortis.

Like many aspiring game developers, it started in the Warcraft III World Editor. After countless hours making maps that were never released and existed purely for personal enjoyment, one thought stayed: the game he wanted most did not exist.

Growing up with Warcraft III, World of Warcraft, Age of Empires, Old School RuneScape, Mount & Blade: Warband, and other games that made worlds feel worth living in, Domormu kept returning to the same idea. If nobody was making that game, he would try to make it himself.

Imperium Mortis has been shaped through more than seven years of off and on work, with the first groundwork beginning in 2016 after more than a decade of ideation. Parts of that work continued while Domormu was deployed to Qatar as Active Duty Air Force. He is now an Air Force veteran.

With a background in psychology and a lifelong fascination with strategy, roleplaying, worldbuilding, and horror, the goal was never to create another power fantasy where the player is destined to save the world. Imperium Mortis asks a different question: what happens when the tension of horror is combined with the consequence of strategy?

Can players care about a character with the same weight they care about the lives they build in The Sims? Can a lost limb, a dead companion, a starving settlement, or a failed expedition become memorable stories rather than temporary setbacks?

Every system, every mechanic, and every decision serves the same goal: creating a world where survival matters, leadership has consequences, and the stories worth remembering are the ones players create for themselves.
