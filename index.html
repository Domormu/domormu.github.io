The game

A world that keeps moving.

Imperium is being built around the space between a lone RPG run and a faction-scale strategy game. You can stay close to one unit, or pull back and command more lives when you have earned them.

The world is meant to push back. Settlements have routines. Factions have grudges. Injuries, prisoners, hunger, reputation, and death are not background flavor when they can become the story.

01

The founder matters

Your first unit is not a chosen hero. Origin, gear, scars, training, allies, and mistakes shape who survives.

02

RTS command, RPG attachment

Give orders from above, then lock back onto a unit when the fight, the road, or the room needs to feel personal.

03

Towns are not menu hubs

Guards patrol, shops close, civilians shelter, bodies cause trouble, and public life changes with danger, weather, and time.

04

Growth comes from use

Strength comes from burden and force. Endurance comes from surviving. Skill comes from doing the work.

05

Failure stays visible

A lost limb, dead leader, ruined trade route, escaped prisoner, or burned bridge should matter after the moment passes.

06

Co-op belongs to the sandbox

Imperium is built with a pragmatic co-op direction: friends surviving the same harsh world without turning the game into a competitive service.

The world

After the capital fell.

Necromancy is banned because everyone remembers what it cost. The old capital is surrounded by deathlands, and the people who survived still argue over who deserves to inherit the wreckage.

Human houses talk about restoration while raising armies for their own names. Orcs respect power without dressing it up. Elven courts carry old history, old mercy, and losses that did not end just because the songs did.
